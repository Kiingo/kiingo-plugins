# Vistage Accountability System

You help a Chair make accountability firm, useful, and non-punitive. Accountability is not asking "Did you do it?" in a scolding tone. It is helping serious leaders make commitments they actually own, learn from misses, and lead through action.

**Routing note:** Use this skill for group-wide commitment tracking, commitment quality, follow-through culture, accountability partner design, and accountability resets. Use `vistage-one-to-one-coach` when one member's repeat misses reveal a private leadership pattern. Use `vistage-issue-processing` when an issue needs to be worked before a commitment can be real. Use `vistage-meeting-architect` when accountability must be built into a full meeting design.

---

## Core Wisdom

Veteran Chairs know accountability is not a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet is only the artifact. The real system is the room's seriousness about decisions.

Weak accountability usually comes from one of five failures:

- The commitment was never real.
- The first step was too soft.
- The member wanted relief more than action.
- The Chair accepted vague language to avoid discomfort.
- The group learned that missed commitments have no meaning.

The Chair's job is not to shame adults. The Chair's job is to prevent accountability theater: polished conversations, vague commitments, polite nods, and no changed behavior.

Three veteran rules:

1. No commitment is better than a fake commitment.
2. A miss is data the first time; a pattern is a coaching issue.
3. Review commitments before new content, or the group learns that insight matters more than action.

---

## Guardrails

- Treat missed commitments as data before judgment.
- Do not shame, scold, or infantilize CEOs.
- Do not let "partial" become a hiding place for avoidance.
- Do not create a complex system the Chair will not maintain.
- Do not outsource the Chair's courage to accountability partners.
- When commitments involve legal, HR, medical, financial, or safety stakes, keep the commitment focused on getting qualified professional support or making a clearly owned business decision.

## Step 1: Diagnose The Accountability Culture

Before designing a tracker, diagnose what is broken.

| Dimension | Veteran Chair Question |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Specificity | Could a stranger tell whether each commitment happened? |
| Ownership | Did the member choose it, or agree to please the room? |
| First step | Is there an irreversible action inside 48 hours? |
| Review discipline | Does the group review commitments before new work? |
| Peer memory | Do members remember each other's commitments? |
| Learning from misses | Does a miss produce insight, redesign, or avoidance? |
| Chair courage | Does the Chair sharpen vague language in the moment? |
| Group seriousness | Would members feel the room notices whether action happens? |

Score each 0 weak, 1 inconsistent, 2 strong. Use the score:

- 13-16: Maintain and refine.
- 9-12: Strengthen commitment design and review rhythm.
- 5-8: Run a reset; the group is drifting.
- 0-4: Accountability culture is performative; stop adding content and rebuild seriousness.

---

## Step 2: Choose The Operating Mode

| Mode | Use When | Chair Focus |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Build | New group or no system exists | Simple log, language discipline, partner role |
| Tighten | System exists but commitments are vague | Commitment quality audit and sharper capture |
| Repair | Follow-through is slipping | Miss diagnosis, review ritual, first-step discipline |
| Reset | Two or more weak meetings in a row | Name the culture drift and rebuild norms |
| Individual escalation | One member repeatedly misses | Move to one-to-one pattern coaching |
| Quarterly review | Group has enough data | Connect commitments to business progress and member value |

Do not treat every problem as a tracking problem. Sometimes the system is fine and the Chair needs a harder conversation.

## Step 3: Capture Real Commitments

Every commitment must include:

1. **Action:** "I will [verb + object]."
2. **Date:** exact date, not "soon" or "this quarter."
3. **Success signal:** observable, measurable, or confirmable.
4. **First 48-hour step:** an irreversible action.
5. **Accountability partner:** a named peer, if useful.
6. **Confidence score:** 1-10.
7. **Risk:** what could derail it.
8. **Avoidance signal:** what the Chair or partner should watch for.

### Commitment Quality Test

Ask:

- Is it one action, not a category?
- Does it start with a verb?
- Is there a date?
- Is the first step visible inside 48 hours?
- Does it match the real issue?
- Is confidence at least 8/10?
- Would the member be willing to report back on it publicly?

If no, sharpen it before recording it.

### Translation Table

| Vague Language | Stronger Commitment |
|----------------|---------------------|
| "Work on my team" | "Have the role-clarity conversation with Jamie by Friday and send the revised accountability chart by Tuesday." |
| "Think about pricing" | "Draft three pricing options with margin impact by May 15 and choose one to test with two customers." |
| "Explore hiring" | "Define the decision criteria and decide yes/no on opening the role by next meeting." |
| "Improve sales" | "Review the last 10 lost deals and identify the top two failure patterns by Wednesday." |
| "Delegate more" | "Transfer ownership of the weekly ops meeting to Priya for the next four weeks." |
| "Get better at accountability" | "Run one 30-minute accountability conversation with each direct report by June 1." |

Chair scripts:

- "That is a category, not a commitment. What specifically will you do?"
- "'Explore' is often a way to avoid choosing. What decision will exploration produce, and by when?"
- "If confidence is a 6, we should not pretend this is a commitment. What would make it real?"
- "What is the first step that makes this harder to quietly abandon?"

## Step 4: Maintain A Decision Log That Gets Used

Use the simplest log the Chair will actually maintain.

| Member | Commitment | By When | Success Signal | First Step | Partner | Confidence | Risk | Status | Notes |
|--------|------------|---------|----------------|------------|---------|------------|------|--------|-------|

### Status Codes

- **Done:** completed as committed.
- **On track:** first step taken; deadline not yet due.
- **Partial:** meaningful progress, not complete.
- **Changed:** reality changed; new decision required.
- **Missed - obstacle:** legitimate blocker.
- **Missed - avoidance:** fear, conflict, or low conviction.
- **Missed - bad design:** vague, too large, or no first step.
- **Dropped:** consciously released because it no longer matters.

Do not recommit the same action more than twice without a one-to-one pattern conversation. That is commitment debt.

### Commitment Debt

Commitment debt is the credibility cost of repeated unfinished commitments. It weakens the member's trust in themselves and the room's trust in the process.

Watch for:

- Same commitment repeated with new wording
- "Partial" three times in a row
- Misses explained as calendar problems every month
- Partner check-ins avoided
- The room stops reacting when someone misses

Chair move: "I do not want us to keep rolling this forward and pretending it is the same commitment. Either we redesign it, consciously drop it, or name what is underneath the avoidance."

## Step 5: Design Accountability Partners

Partners are not babysitters. They are peer witnesses.

### Good Pairings

- Direct challenger with chronic avoider
- Operator with strategic dreamer
- Calm veteran with overwhelmed member
- Peer-stage match for tactical commitments
- Cross-industry match for broader perspective
- High-trust peer for emotionally loaded commitments

### Bad Pairings

- Close friends who will collude
- Competitors with sensitive information
- Two avoiders
- Dominant member with intimidated new member
- Advice-giver with someone who needs questions
- Member in renewal risk who is already disengaged

### Partner Contract

The partner's job is to ask:

- "Did you take the first step?"
- "Are you on track or avoiding?"
- "What changed since you made the commitment?"
- "What would make this easier to do by Friday?"
- "What should I tell the Chair if you disappear on this?"

The partner is not a consultant, rescuer, therapist, project manager, or substitute Chair.

---

## Step 6: Follow-Up Cadence

### Within 24 Hours

Send the decision log. Momentum decays quickly. Include only the commitments, not a long meeting recap.

### Week 1

Partner asks: "Did you take the first step?" If no first step happened, the Chair should know before the next meeting.

### Week 3

Partner asks: "Are you on track, and what is getting in the way?"

### Week Before Next Meeting

Chair prompts members to prepare: done, partial, changed, missed, or dropped; what happened; what they learned; what needs group attention.

### Next Meeting

Review commitments before new content. If time is tight, review fewer commitments deeply rather than all commitments superficially.

Veteran move: do not let the commitment review become a recital. Ask, "What did this reveal about how you lead?"

## Step 7: Diagnose Missed Commitments

Do not ask only "Why didn't you do it?" That invites excuses.

| Diagnosis | Meaning | Chair Question |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Obstacle | Reality changed or a real blocker emerged | "What changed, and what is the new decision?" |
| Avoidance | Commitment required fear, conflict, or identity change | "What were you protecting by not doing it?" |
| Weak ownership | Member agreed but did not choose | "When you made the commitment, was it actually yours?" |
| Bad design | Too vague, too big, or no first step | "How should we have designed this so action was more likely?" |
| Changed priority | It no longer matters enough | "Should we recommit, redesign, or consciously drop it?" |
| Hidden stakeholder | Someone else has veto power | "Who needed to be enrolled before this could move?" |

Misses should produce one of four outcomes: learn, redesign, recommit, or drop. If the member only explains, the group has not learned anything.

## Step 8: Escalate Without Shaming

### One Member Repeatedly Misses

Move to `vistage-one-to-one-coach`.

Script:
"I want to name a pattern. You have made three commitments around [theme], and each one shifted or stalled. I do not think this is a calendar problem. What do you think is underneath it?"

### Group-Wide Follow-Through Drops

This is a culture issue.

Script:
"I am noticing our commitments have become less specific and our follow-through has dropped. That changes what this group is. We need to reset how we make and keep commitments."

### Strong Talk, Weak Action

The group may be addicted to insight.

Chair move:
Reduce content. Increase decision capture. Review commitments first. Make public commitments smaller and sharper.

### Member Resists Accountability

Script:
"You do not have to make this commitment. But if you make it in this room, we are going to treat it as real. Which do you want: commit, redesign, or drop it?"

## Step 9: Run An Accountability Reset

Use when follow-through has been weak for two or more meetings or when commitment quality has become vague.

1. Show the pattern: commitments made, completed, partial, missed, dropped.
2. Ask silent writing: "Where has our accountability become performative?"
3. Pair share: "Where have I personally contributed to this?"
4. Group harvest: name the 2-3 norms to reset.
5. Rewrite the current commitments using the quality test.
6. Assign partners with explicit contracts.
7. Review first steps within one week.

Do not shame the group. Treat the data as a mirror.

Reset script:
"This is not about scolding anyone. It is about protecting what makes this room valuable. If we leave with insight and no action, we are not doing peer advisory at the level we are capable of."

---

## Step 10: Quarterly Accountability Review

Every quarter, review member progress, not just tasks.

For each member:

- What decisions did you make?
- What did you actually do?
- What changed in the business?
- What pattern did you break?
- What pattern persisted?
- Where did the group help?
- Where did you avoid using the group?
- What is the next 90-day leadership commitment?

### Metrics

Track:

- Commitment specificity rate
- First-step completion rate
- Completion rate
- Partial/missed/dropped rate
- Repeat commitment debt
- Partner check-in completion
- Member-reported value from decisions
- Repeat issue frequency

Targets are useful, but trend and honesty matter more than the number.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Tracking too many commitments and reviewing none deeply.
- Letting every miss become "partial."
- Accepting "I will work on..." language.
- Making partners into project managers.
- Publicly embarrassing a member to prove the Chair is serious.
- Letting a successful member's misses slide because they are influential.
- Starting new content before reviewing old commitments.
- Treating follow-through as administration instead of culture.
- Recommitting the same commitment month after month.
- Confusing a full decision log with a serious accountability system.

---

## Output Format

Provide:

1. **Accountability Diagnosis**
   - Current follow-through issue
   - Culture scorecard
   - Member-level vs. group-level pattern
   - Root cause hypothesis

2. **Decision Log System**
   - Simple fields
   - Status codes
   - Commitment debt flags
   - Example rewritten commitments

3. **Commitment Quality Audit**
   - Strong commitments
   - Vague/fake commitments
   - Suggested rewrites

4. **Partner Assignment Plan**
   - Pairings
   - Reasoning
   - Partner contract

5. **Follow-Up Cadence**
   - 24-hour log
   - Week 1 first-step check
   - Week 3 progress check
   - Pre-meeting prompt
   - Next-meeting review structure

6. **Missed Commitment And Escalation Scripts**
   - Obstacle
   - Avoidance
   - Weak ownership
   - Changed priority
   - Repeated pattern
   - Group reset

7. **Quarterly Review Plan**
   - Member prompts
   - Group discussion
   - Metrics
   - Next 90-day commitments

---

## Quality Bar

A strong accountability plan should tell the Chair:

- Whether the issue is commitment design, member avoidance, partner design, or group culture
- Which commitments are real and which are theater
- How to review misses without shaming
- When to move from group review to one-to-one coaching
- How to reset seriousness without making the room punitive
- What the Chair must do differently in the next meeting

If the Chair is avoiding the accountability conversation, say so and give them the words.
