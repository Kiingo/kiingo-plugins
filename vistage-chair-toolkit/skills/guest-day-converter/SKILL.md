# Vistage Guest Day Converter

You help a Chair use guest day as a real fit test. Guest day is not a pitch. It is where the prospect experiences the product: the caliber of the room, the trust in the room, and whether they can imagine being honest there.

**Routing note:** Use this skill for prospect visits to a live group meeting. Use `vistage-prospect-signals` for the evidence layer, `vistage-prospect-intel` before inviting the guest, `vistage-outreach-campaign` for pre-visit outreach, `vistage-objection-battlecards` for stated objections, `vistage-stalled-prospect-reviver` when a guest defers, stalls, or needs a relationship-safe re-entry plan, `vistage-referral-activation-engine` when a guest, referrer, or passed prospect should be asked for introductions, and `vistage-member-onboarding` after they join.

---

## Core Wisdom

The guest is evaluating three things:

1. **Caliber**: Are these people sharp enough to help me?
2. **Trust**: Is this room safe enough for real issues?
3. **Self-recognition**: Can I see myself being honest here?

The group is also evaluating:

1. Will this person add to the room?
2. Will they protect confidentiality?
3. Will they contribute without dominating?
4. Will their presence increase or reduce candor?

Fit matters more than close rate. A wrong-fit guest who becomes a member can damage trust, referrals, and renewal.

---

## Step 1: Decide Whether To Invite The Guest

Do not use guest day as a fishing expedition. Invite when the Chair has enough confidence that the prospect could be a fit.

### Invite When

- The prospect meets the group's scale and complexity threshold.
- There is no unresolved competitive conflict.
- The prospect shows learning orientation.
- The Chair can explain why this person would add to the room.
- The current group is healthy enough to host without performing.

### Do Not Invite Yet When

- The prospect is mainly curious but not serious.
- They demand member names or confidential details.
- They show status-seeking or dismissive behavior.
- The group is in a low-trust moment.
- The prospect has a direct competitive conflict.
- The Chair is hoping the group will sell them because the Chair has not qualified them.

### The Guest Readiness Question

"If this person joined three months from now, what would happen to the room?"

If the answer is unclear, do more qualification before guest day.

---

## Step 2: Prep The Guest

The guest needs enough structure to participate well without feeling managed.

### Guest Prep Call

Cover:

- What the day is and is not
- Confidentiality expectations
- How much they should share
- How members will interact
- Whether they will observe, participate, or bring a bounded issue
- What happens after the meeting

### Guest Prep Script

"I want you to experience the room honestly. This is not a showcase, and I have asked the group not to perform for you. You will see real business owners working real issues. Confidentiality matters: anything you hear stays in the room. Your job is to participate enough to feel the group, ask good questions, and notice whether this is a room where you could bring real decisions."

### Set The Frame

Tell them:

- You do not need to impress the room.
- You do need to be real enough for the room to know you.
- Ask questions before offering advice.
- If something feels uncomfortable, notice it. That may be where the value is.

---

## Step 3: Prep The Group

The group must not sell, posture, or soften the meeting for the guest.

### Group Prep Note

"We have a guest joining. Please do not perform for them or sell the group. Run the room the way we normally run it. Protect confidentiality. Welcome them warmly, ask real questions, and let the quality of the work speak for itself. I am evaluating fit as much as interest."

### What To Tell Members

- Do not pitch Vistage.
- Do not exaggerate outcomes.
- Do not disclose confidential member history to prove value.
- Do not make the guest the center of the meeting.
- Do model the norms: candor, questions, specificity, commitment.

### Watch For Group Distortion

If members become unusually polished, polite, or performative, name it lightly:
"Let's run this like a real meeting. The most useful thing for our guest is to see the room as it actually works."

---

## Step 4: Choose The Guest Participation Mode

### Observer Plus Light Participation

Use when:
- The group is processing sensitive issues.
- The prospect is early in trust.
- The Chair needs to assess fit before deeper involvement.

Guest role:
- Introduce briefly
- Participate in exercises
- Ask clarifying questions
- Avoid giving long advice

### Full Exercise Participant

Use when:
- The agenda has a general business theme.
- The guest has enough context to contribute.
- Confidentiality risk is manageable.

Guest role:
- Complete pre-work if appropriate
- Join trios or pairs
- Share a relevant decision or insight

### Bounded Guest Issue

Use only when:
- The prospect has a clear, safe issue.
- The Chair has prepared it in advance.
- The group has enough trust to process it well.
- Current members will not feel displaced.

Issue must be:
- Real but not explosive
- Specific
- Decision-oriented
- Safe to discuss with non-members in the room

## Step 5: Design The Conversion Moments

Guest day conversion is not one closing line. It is a sequence of moments.

### Before The Meeting

The guest should know:
- Why they were invited
- What to watch for
- What participation looks like
- What confidentiality requires

### During The Meeting

The guest should experience:
- Sharp questions
- Real vulnerability
- Useful challenge
- Follow-through culture
- Members who are peers, not audience

### After The Meeting

The Chair should debrief while the experience is fresh.

Best first question:
"What did you notice about the room?"

Then:
- "Where did you see value?"
- "What felt uncomfortable or surprising?"
- "Could you imagine bringing your real issues here?"
- "Where did the room feel above, at, or below your level?"
- "What would you need to know before deciding whether this is a fit?"

Do not start with "Do you want to join?" Start by learning how they experienced the room.

---

## Step 6: Fit Assessment After Guest Day

Rate both interest and fit.

| Dimension | Question |
|----------|----------|
| Caliber fit | Did they respect the room, and did the room respect them? |
| Candor fit | Did they show capacity for honesty without oversharing? |
| Contribution fit | Did they ask useful questions or only consume? |
| Humility/confidence | Did they balance competence with openness? |
| Confidentiality | Did they treat privacy seriously? |
| Energy effect | Did the room get better or tighter with them present? |
| Commitment fit | Can they actually attend and participate? |
| Conflict risk | Would anyone withhold because they are in the room? |

### Outcome Categories

**Strong fit, ready to invite**
They saw value, the room respected them, and no major concerns surfaced.

**Possible fit, needs one more conversation**
Interest exists, but one concern needs testing: time, fit, vulnerability, caliber, or conflict.

**Good person, wrong timing**
Preserve relationship. Set a specific follow-up horizon, then use `vistage-stalled-prospect-reviver` if the Chair later needs to re-open the conversation.

**Wrong fit**
Exit gracefully. Protect the room.

---

## Step 7: Follow-Up Strategy

### If Strong Fit

Email within 24 hours:

"I appreciated how you engaged yesterday, especially [specific moment]. My read is that there could be a strong fit here, both in what you would get from the room and what you would add to it. I'd like to talk through what you noticed, answer questions, and decide together whether the next step makes sense."

### If They Need To Think

"Of course. This should be a considered decision. To make the thinking useful, what specifically are you weighing: the time, the investment, the group fit, or whether you are ready to bring real issues into a room like this?"

### If Not A Fit

"I appreciated you spending the day with us. After reflecting on the room and what you are navigating, I do not think this is the right fit right now. I respect you enough to say that directly. I would still be glad to stay connected and be useful where I can."

### If Group Has Concerns

Do not ignore them. Ask:
- What specifically concerned you?
- Is this a fit issue or a discomfort issue?
- Would their presence reduce candor?
- Is there a way to address the concern, or is it disqualifying?

---

## Common Failure Modes

**The Group Performs**
Members sell the value and soften the work. The guest sees a pitch, not a peer room.

**The Guest Over-Advises**
They try to prove value by giving advice. Chair redirects: "Let's stay in questions first."

**The Chair Over-Closes**
The Chair pushes membership before understanding the guest's experience.

**The Guest Is Too Central**
Current members feel displaced. The meeting loses its real purpose.

**The Chair Ignores Group Signals**
Members see fit concerns, but the Chair wants the seat. This damages trust.

---

## Output Format

Provide:

1. **Guest Day Readiness Assessment**
   - Invite now, qualify more, defer, or decline
   - Fit risks
   - Group risks

2. **Guest Prep Plan**
   - Prep call agenda
   - Confidentiality script
   - Participation guidance

3. **Group Prep Note**
   - What members should do
   - What members should avoid

4. **Meeting Integration Plan**
   - Participation mode
   - Agenda moments
   - Chair interventions

5. **Post-Meeting Debrief Script**
   - Questions
   - Objection tests
   - Fit assessment

6. **Follow-Up Message**
   - Strong fit, maybe, defer, or decline version
