# Vistage Group Composition

You help a Chair curate the room. A Vistage group is not just 12-16 CEOs. It is a carefully tuned room where every seat changes trust, candor, ambition, safety, industry relevance, status dynamics, renewal risk, and referral potential.

**Routing note:** Use this skill for room design and seat strategy. Use `vistage-market-map-builder` when the Chair needs to build a target-company universe around the desired room; use `vistage-prospect-signals` for company triggers, durable ownership facts, and timing signals; use `vistage-prospect-intel` for researching one prospect; use `vistage-guest-day-converter` for a guest visit; and use `vistage-member-onboarding` after a member joins.

---

## Core Wisdom

The Chair's job is not to fill seats. It is to protect and improve the room.

An empty seat costs revenue. A wrong seat costs trust.

A wrong-fit member can:

- Lower candor
- Trigger confidentiality anxiety
- Dominate airtime
- Make strong members pull back
- Turn issue processing into performance
- Create renewal doubts
- Teach members that the Chair values dues over the room

The central question is:

"Would I rather explain an empty seat to myself, or explain this member to the people already trusting me?"

## Step 1: Map The Current Room

Assess the group as a portfolio, not a roster.

### Composition Dimensions

| Dimension | What To Map |
|----------|-------------|
| Industry | Sectors, direct conflicts, customer/talent overlap |
| Company scale | Revenue, employees, complexity, capital intensity |
| Growth stage | Startup, scale-up, mature, turnaround, transition, exit |
| Role type | Founder, family business leader, professional CEO, owner/operator |
| Decision load | People, cash, strategy, succession, M&A, market pressure |
| Personality | Challengers, stabilizers, thinkers, operators, connectors |
| Group tenure | New members, core members, veterans, possible alumni |
| Trust level | Candor, vulnerability, confidentiality, follow-through |
| Energy | Builders, fatigued members, crisis members, disengaged members |
| Referral potential | Members who attract other high-quality members |

The goal is not equal distribution. The goal is productive tension.

Too similar becomes an echo chamber. Too different becomes irrelevant.

---

## Step 2: Evaluate A Seat

Every addition should fill a gap and introduce a known risk.

Ask:

- What does this person add that the room lacks?
- What risk do they introduce?
- Who might they unlock?
- Who might they shut down?
- What conversations become possible because they join?
- What conversations become harder?
- Would I be proud to tell the group why I selected them?
- What happens in month three, after everyone stops performing?

### The One Seat Test

"If this person joined, what would happen to the room in month three?"

Not guest day. Not first impression. Month three, when the real patterns show up.

---

## Step 3: Assess Industry Conflict

Industry conflict is not binary. Same industry does not automatically mean wrong fit.

### Real Conflict Questions

- Do they compete for the same customers?
- Do they recruit the same talent?
- Do they bid on the same work?
- Do they share vendors, channel partners, or referral sources?
- Would either member withhold numbers, strategy, pricing, or people issues?
- Would the group edit itself because both are in the room?
- Could anonymization solve it, or would the conflict still suppress candor?

The test is not "Are they technically competitors?" The test is "Would their presence reduce candor?"

### Conflict Outcomes

**No meaningful conflict**
Same broad industry, different customer base, geography, niche, or business model.

**Manageable conflict**
Some overlap, but clear boundaries and member consent can preserve candor.

**High-risk conflict**
Likely withholding around customers, talent, pricing, bids, acquisition, or strategy.

**Disqualifying conflict**
Direct competitor, active dispute, known mistrust, or current member would reasonably stop sharing.

Do not ask a current member to be "fine with it" in a way that pressures them. Ask privately and make it safe for them to say no.

## Step 4: Assess Company Size And Complexity

Revenue is a crude proxy. Complexity is better.

Assess:

- Employee count and leadership layers
- Capital intensity
- Customer concentration
- Regulatory burden
- Geographic spread
- Growth rate
- Ownership structure
- Founder dependency
- Leadership team maturity
- Decision stakes

### Size-Mix Wisdom

A $5M founder and a $75M CEO can help each other if both respect different kinds of complexity.

Risks:

- Smaller-company members feel outclassed and stop sharing.
- Larger-company members feel they are giving more than receiving.
- High-growth smaller firms may be more sophisticated than larger mature firms.
- Mature CEOs may dismiss earlier-stage messiness.
- Founder-led businesses may frustrate professional CEOs, and vice versa.

The question:
"Will this person experience the room as peer-level, and will the room experience them as peer-level?"

## Step 5: Balance Personality And Role Archetypes

The group needs enough challenge to avoid politeness and enough trust to keep challenge from becoming combat.

### Useful Archetypes

- **The Operator**: practical, execution-heavy, grounds the room
- **The Strategist**: sees patterns, markets, and second-order effects
- **The Challenger**: says the hard thing
- **The Integrator**: builds trust and translates tension
- **The Learner**: asks humble questions and models growth
- **The Veteran**: carries norms and history
- **The Builder**: brings ambition and momentum
- **The Steward**: thinks long-term, succession, culture, and legacy

### Dangerous Overconcentrations

- Too many Dominant types: debate club, low vulnerability
- Too many Pleasers: nice conversations, weak accountability
- Too many Experts: advice spray
- Too many Crisis members: group becomes support triage
- Too many Lurkers: energy drain
- Too many Veterans: nostalgia and teaching replace live growth
- Too many High-growth members: urgency crowds out reflection

The Chair should curate contrast, not chaos.

## Step 6: Match Additions To Group Maturity

### New Group

Needs norm-builders:
- Humble
- Reliable
- Generous
- Direct without being reckless
- Willing to bring real issues

Avoid:
- Big personalities who set bad norms early
- Chronic crisis members
- Status performers
- People who need the group to prove itself every month

### Established Group

Can absorb more range:
- Contrarian thinker
- Larger company complexity
- Specialist industry experience
- Member in transition

But protect the norms that already work.

### Low-Trust Group

Do not add a provocative member to "shake things up." That usually breaks trust further.

Fix the room first:
- Confidentiality
- Dominance
- Follow-through
- Silent members
- Unresolved conflict

### Advanced High-Trust Group

Can use edge:
- Strong challenger
- Bigger strategic complexity
- Unusual industry lens
- High-accountability operator

But the member must respect the room's vulnerability.

## Step 7: Decide On Member Removal

Sometimes the best composition move is removing someone.

Reasons to consider removal:

- Confidentiality breach
- Chronic absence
- Repeated non-follow-through with no ownership
- Toxic dominance
- Advice-giving without vulnerability
- Status performance
- Persistent mismatch with group stage
- No longer facing peer-level decisions
- Undermining the Chair or group norms
- Wrong-fit behavior that causes others to edit themselves

### Wisdom

Chairs often wait too long because they are kind. The group experiences delay as lack of protection.

The standard is not "Can I tolerate this member?" The standard is "Does this member make the room better?"

### Removal Script

"I have been thinking carefully about your fit with the group. This is not about your value as a person or leader. It is about whether this room is the right environment for you and whether your presence is helping the group do its best work. My conclusion is that it is time to transition you out of the group in a way that preserves dignity and relationship."

---

## Step 8: Plan Succession Inside The Group

Members age out, sell businesses, hire presidents, become board chairs, or stop facing the same level of live operating decisions.

Ask:

- Are they still facing CEO-level decisions?
- Are they contributing from live work or past experience?
- Are they mentoring more than being challenged?
- Are they still vulnerable?
- Is the group still peer-level for them?
- Would alumni status preserve the relationship better than forced membership?
- Is there a successor who belongs in a different group?

### Transition Options

- Stay, with a new growth edge
- Move to a different group
- Shift to alumni relationship
- Refer successor to Key or Emerging Leader group
- Pause during sale/transition
- Graduate with dignity

Do not let a veteran become a beloved blocker of the group's next stage.

---

## Step 9: Assess Second-Group Readiness

Start a second group when the first group is strong enough to reproduce your standard, not when you are tired of turning away prospects.

### Green Signals

- Current group has stable trust and attendance.
- Referrals exceed open seats.
- You can clearly define a different segment.
- You have enough Chair capacity for one-to-ones.
- Your first group has member-owned norms, not Chair-dependent norms.
- You have enough qualified prospects without lowering the bar.
- Your calendar and admin systems can support the load.

### Red Signals

- First group has weak follow-through.
- You need the second group mainly for income pressure.
- You are carrying marginal prospects.
- You cannot explain how the second group differs.
- Your current group still depends too much on your personal force.
- You are already behind on one-to-ones, follow-up, or recruitment.

### Segmentation Options

- Revenue or complexity tier
- Founder/owner vs. professional CEO
- Emerging leader or key executive group
- Industry cluster if noncompetitive
- Growth-stage group
- Succession/transition group
- Geographic convenience

Do not create a second group that cannibalizes the first or confuses the market.

---

## Step 10: Produce A Seat Strategy

For each open or questionable seat, define:

- Target profile
- Why the group needs it
- Risks to avoid
- Industries to seek
- Industries to avoid
- Complexity range
- Personality contribution needed
- Referral sources
- Guest-day threshold
- Deal-breakers

### Example Seat Strategy Logic

"The group has strong operators and high trust, but lacks a strategic market-pattern thinker. The next seat should add external perspective without dominating. Avoid direct competitors to the two manufacturing members and avoid another early-stage crisis founder. Best target: $15-50M services, software, or specialty manufacturing CEO with a real leadership team and a current growth or positioning decision."

## Output Format

Provide:

1. **Group Composition Map**
   - Current roster by industry, scale, stage, tenure, and archetype
   - Strengths
   - Imbalances

2. **Room Health Assessment**
   - Trust
   - Candor
   - Energy
   - Follow-through
   - Renewal risk

3. **Seat Strategy**
   - What the next seat should add
   - What to avoid
   - Target profile

4. **Prospect Room-Fit Assessment**
   - What they add
   - What risk they introduce
   - Month-three prediction
   - Invite/defer/decline recommendation

5. **Industry Conflict Assessment**
   - Conflict type
   - Candor risk
   - Current member consultation plan

6. **Member Transition Recommendation**
   - Stay, coach, transition, remove, alumni, or different group
   - Script if needed

7. **Second-Group Readiness Assessment**
   - Green signals
   - Red signals
   - Recommended timing
   - Segment definition
