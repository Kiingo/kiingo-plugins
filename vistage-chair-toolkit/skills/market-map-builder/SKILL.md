# Vistage Market Map Builder

You help a Vistage Chair move from "I need more members" to a disciplined target universe. The job is not to produce random leads. The job is to define the room, identify where the right CEOs already exist in the market, and create a practical recruiting map.

This skill sits upstream of `vistage-chair-authority-builder`, `vistage-public-trigger-monitor`, `vistage-referral-source-briefs`, `vistage-referral-activation-engine`, `vistage-prospect-signals`, `vistage-prospect-intel`, `vistage-outreach-campaign`, and `vistage-recruitment-playbook`.

**Routing note:** Use this skill for market definition, target segments, company lists, and first-pass prioritization. Use `vistage-chair-authority-builder` after this skill identifies credibility lanes that should become positioning, content, talks, or advisor assets. Use `vistage-public-trigger-monitor` after this skill identifies companies or segments the Chair wants to watch over time. Use `vistage-referral-source-briefs` for specific members, advisors, alumni, or connectors before asking for introductions. Use `vistage-referral-activation-engine` after this skill identifies source maps, trust paths, or named intro opportunities. Use `vistage-prospect-signals` after this skill identifies named companies that deserve a signal scan.

---

## Core Wisdom

A Chair starting from scratch does not need "more leads." They need a point of view about the room they are building.

The central question is:

"What kind of room am I trying to build, and where do those people already exist in my market?"

Recruiting gets better when the Chair thinks in this order:

1. Starting situation
2. Room strategy
3. Seat thesis
4. Target company universe
5. Segment priorities
6. Company scoring
7. Access strategy
8. Decision-maker verification
9. Signal scans
10. Outreach and conversations

Do not begin with outreach. Begin with room design.

## Starting Mode Selector

First classify what the Chair is actually doing. Different modes produce different market maps.

| Mode | Signals | Primary Output |
|------|---------|----------------|
| New Chair / first group | No roster, no pipeline, unclear market thesis | Founding-room thesis, target segments, 50-company longlist, first 30-day plan |
| Existing group with open seats | Current room exists, 1-5 seats open | Gap-based seat thesis, conflict watchlist, priority targets by room need |
| Second group | One healthy group, desire to expand | Distinct group thesis, market separation, member/referral leverage plan |
| Stalled recruiting | Some lists or activity, few conversions | Pipeline diagnosis, access-path repair, segment pruning |
| Niche/specialty group | Industry, role, or stage focus | Density check, conflict rules, credibility and source map |

If the Chair does not specify, infer the likely mode and say the assumption.

## Step 1: Define The Room

Ask only the missing questions needed to start.

Essential:

- Geography and realistic travel radius
- Group type: CEO, key executive, emerging leader, industry-specific, or mixed
- Desired company size or complexity
- Current open seats or desired founding group size
- Industries to favor or avoid
- Any known member/prospect conflicts

Helpful:

- Chair background and credibility lanes
- Current referral sources
- Existing target list, even if messy
- Desired group personality: growth-heavy, family-business, operators, succession, high-growth, etc.
- Meeting location and cadence

If the Chair is starting from zero, use defaults:

- Target owners, CEOs, presidents, founders, managing partners, or successors.
- Prioritize privately held, founder-led, family-owned, PE-backed, or professionalized SMB/mid-market companies.
- Look for enough operating complexity that peers matter: employees, leadership layers, locations, capital intensity, customer concentration, succession, or growth pressure.
- Avoid direct competitors in the same room.

### Seat Thesis Builder

Before listing companies, define the kind of seat the Chair is trying to fill or create.

Provide:

- **Must-haves**: role authority, complexity, geography, meeting availability, values fit.
- **Nice-to-haves**: segment, growth stage, ownership type, functional expertise, referral value.
- **Disqualifiers**: direct competition, chronic unavailability, reputation risk, low coachability, dues strain.
- **Missing perspectives**: what the current or intended room lacks.
- **Contribution hypothesis**: what this member would teach the room.
- **Challenge hypothesis**: what the room would help this member face.

Seat thesis test:

"Would I be proud to explain to the room why I selected this person?"

If not, the target is not ready for pursuit.

### Chair Credibility Lanes

Map where the Chair has natural authority. A Chair can recruit outside these lanes, but early market maps should favor them.

Credibility can come from:

- Former industry or function
- CEO/operator experience
- M&A, succession, sales, talent, finance, or scaling expertise
- Current member success stories
- Speaker topics
- Board, nonprofit, chamber, or community roles
- Advisor relationships
- Geographic reputation

For each target segment, note whether the Chair has high, medium, or low credibility and what proof point supports it.

### Room Definition Output

Create:

- **Target member profile**
- **Minimum complexity threshold**
- **Geographic boundary**
- **Preferred segments**
- **Excluded segments**
- **Room-building thesis**

Example:

"This group should recruit owners/CEOs of privately held companies with 25-250 employees in San Diego County, with a bias toward multi-location service businesses, family-owned manufacturers, and founder-led B2B services firms. The room should be operator-heavy, growth-oriented, and strong enough on candor to support succession and people-decision work."

## Step 2: Choose Target Segments

Do not map the entire market equally. Pick segments where Vistage pain is likely and where the Chair can credibly access prospects.

### High-Value Segments

| Segment | Why It Often Fits Vistage | Watch-Out |
|---------|---------------------------|-----------|
| Founder-led growth companies | Founder bottleneck, scaling, leadership team gaps | Founder may resist vulnerability |
| Family businesses | Succession, governance, role clarity, family/business boundaries | Sensitive confidentiality and family dynamics |
| PE-backed / investor-backed companies | Professionalization, growth pressure, reporting cadence | Decision authority may sit above local CEO |
| Multi-location operators | Consistency, delegation, people systems, management layers | Operators may feel too busy for full-day meetings |
| Manufacturers / distributors | Margin, labor, process, customer concentration, succession | Direct industry conflicts can be real |
| Construction / trades / field services | Labor, scheduling, cash flow, owner dependency, estimating risk | Often LinkedIn-passive; use local reputation and referrals |
| Professional services firms | Partner dynamics, pricing, talent, succession, rainmaker dependency | Managing partner may not identify as CEO |
| Healthcare / dental / veterinary groups | Regulation, staffing, consolidation, multi-site complexity | Compliance and ownership structure can complicate fit |
| Franchise / franchisee operators | Growth systems, unit economics, manager layers | Brand decisions may be constrained |
| B2B SaaS / tech firms | Growth, capital, talent, GTM, product-market decisions | May already have investors/advisors; differentiate peer room |

### Segment Selection Logic

Score each segment 1-5:

- **Density**: Are there enough target companies locally?
- **Pain fit**: Do leaders in this segment face decisions peers can help with?
- **Chair credibility**: Can the Chair speak credibly to this segment?
- **Access paths**: Are there members, advisors, associations, or events nearby?
- **Room value**: Would this segment add useful perspective to the desired room?
- **Conflict manageability**: Can competitors be separated or screened?

Pick 3-5 priority segments. A Chair starting from scratch needs focus.

### Market Density Check

Check whether the chosen segment is viable in the geography.

Classify:

- **Too narrow**: fewer than 20 plausible companies or heavy conflict overlap.
- **Focused**: 20-75 plausible companies and clear access paths.
- **Broad**: 75+ companies, requires sub-segmentation.
- **Noisy**: many companies, but weak fit or unclear decision makers.

If too narrow, broaden by adjacent segment, geography, company size, or ownership type. If too broad, narrow by ownership, complexity, employee count, growth stage, or access path.

## Step 3: Build The Company Universe

Identify companies before people. People change roles; companies reveal market structure.

### Public Source Menu

Use sources appropriate to the market: local business journal lists, Inc. 5000/regional growth lists, chambers, economic development directories, industry associations, trade directories, award lists, commercial real estate announcements, facility openings, PE portfolio pages, franchise/operator lists, event sponsor/speaker rosters, nonprofit board pages, and public customer/vendor ecosystem pages.

### Search Patterns

Use combinations like:

- `"[city]" "largest private companies" CEO`
- `"[city]" "fastest growing companies" founder`
- `"[city]" "family owned business" president`
- `"[industry]" "[city]" association members`
- `"[city]" "manufacturer" "president"`
- `"[city]" "business journal" "CEO" "award"`
- `"[city]" "Inc. 5000" company`
- `"[industry]" "private equity portfolio" "[state]"`

Do not scrape private platforms or automate LinkedIn. If a public directory blocks access or terms are unclear, skip it.

### Longlist Rule

For a new Chair:

- Build a 50-company longlist if possible.
- Narrow to 20 priority companies.
- Run `vistage-public-trigger-monitor` for the watchlist and `vistage-prospect-signals` on the top 10.
- Identify trust paths for the top 5.

## Step 4: Score Companies

Use a simple score. Precision theater is worse than a useful rough ranking.

Score 1-5:

| Dimension | What To Look For |
|-----------|------------------|
| Fit | Company size, complexity, seniority of likely leader, private-company relevance |
| Timing | Expansion, leadership change, acquisition, succession, hiring, funding, market pressure |
| Access | Member referral, advisor path, event path, shared community, public contact path |
| Room Value | Perspective, operating experience, industry diversity, maturity, peer relevance |
| Conflict Risk | Direct competition, customer/talent overlap, current member sensitivity |
| Readiness Hypothesis | Evidence they may value peer counsel now |

### Priority Tiers

- **A target**: high fit, timing or access exists, likely room value, manageable conflict.
- **B target**: good fit but weak timing or access.
- **C target**: interesting but unclear fit, weak access, or unresolved conflict.
- **Defer/pass**: low complexity, poor room fit, direct conflict, or likely taker profile.

### Scoring Wisdom

- A perfect-fit company with no trust path may still be worth slow nurture.
- A medium-fit company with a strong member referral may deserve immediate attention.
- Do not let a hot trigger event override poor room fit.
- One wrong-fit member can damage renewal, referrals, and candor.

### Access-Weighted Priority

After scoring, adjust priority by access. Chairs have limited time; warm paths matter.

| Access Level | Meaning | Recruiting Move |
|--------------|---------|-----------------|
| Warm | Current member, trusted advisor, alumni, or strong community tie can intro | Pursue now |
| Lukewarm | Shared event, association, board, speaker audience, or weak mutual connection | Build familiarity first |
| Cold but specific | Strong company fit and real public signal, but no relationship path | Use value-first direct outreach only after signal scan |
| Cold and generic | No relationship path and no specific timing reason | Nurture or skip |

Do not let the map become a fantasy list of impressive names. Every A target needs a plausible first conversation path.

## Step 5: Find Likely Decision Makers

Only after the company is worth pursuing, identify the person.

Likely roles:

- CEO
- Owner
- Founder
- President
- Managing Partner
- Successor / next-generation leader
- Regional GM or division president
- COO/CFO for key executive groups

Verify through public sources:

- Company leadership page
- Press releases
- Podcast/interview bios
- Business journal articles
- Award pages
- Association boards
- Public filings or licensing pages where relevant

Avoid guessing. If the right person is unclear, output "decision maker unknown" and list the verification steps.

## Step 6: Build Trust Paths

A company is not a prospect until there is a plausible path to conversation.

### Trust Path Types

| Path | How To Use It |
|------|---------------|
| Member referral | Ask a current member for a specific intro, not "do you know anyone?" |
| Advisor referral | CPA, attorney, banker, insurance broker, wealth advisor, consultant |
| Event path | Invite to roundtable, breakfast, panel, or guest-safe discussion |
| Content path | Send a relevant insight tied to a real signal |
| Community path | Nonprofit board, chamber, trade association, civic leadership |
| Alumni/former member | Ask for perspective or referral if relationship is positive |
| Direct path | Use only when research is strong and message is specific |

For each priority company, identify up to three trust paths. Rank them by warmth and realism.

### Referral Source Map

Map the people around the target companies, not just the companies themselves.

Source categories:

- Current members
- Former members and alumni
- CPAs and fractional CFOs
- Attorneys and estate/succession counsel
- Bankers and SBA lenders
- Wealth advisors and insurance brokers
- Business brokers and M&A advisors
- PE operating partners and independent sponsors
- Chamber, EO/YPO/industry association leaders
- Commercial real estate brokers
- Nonprofit board connectors
- Speakers, consultants, and coaches serving the segment

For each source type, output:

- Why they have access
- What kind of prospect they likely know
- What the Chair should ask for
- Why referring helps them
- Follow-up cadence

### Access Strategy: From Map To First Conversation

For the top targets, produce a next-action matrix:

| Company | Best path | Person to ask | Specific ask | Backup path | This week action |
|---------|-----------|---------------|--------------|-------------|------------------|

The ask must be specific. Avoid "Do you know anyone?" Use:

- "Do you know the owner or president at [Company] well enough to make a thoughtful intro?"
- "Which CEOs in [segment] do you respect enough that you would sit in a confidential room with them?"
- "Who in your advisor network serves companies like [target segment]?"

### Roundtable/Event Bridge

When no warm path exists for several good-fit companies, create proximity before outreach.

Suggest 1-2 event concepts matched to the top segments:

- Topic title
- Target attendee profile
- Why the topic matters now
- Which current members or advisors could invite guests
- Guest-safe discussion questions
- Follow-up path after the event

Good roundtable topics are decision-based, not promotional: "The people decision every founder avoids too long," "Scaling from owner-led to leadership-team-led," "Succession without losing the business's soul," or "Margins, labor, and pricing in field-service businesses."

## Step 7: Produce The Market Map

Output a practical map, not a research essay.

Default output:

1. **Room-building thesis**
2. **Seat thesis**
3. **Chair credibility lanes**
4. **Priority segments and market density check**
5. **Source plan**
6. **Target-company longlist**
7. **Top 20 priority companies**
8. **Likely decision makers**
9. **Trust paths and referral source map**
10. **Conflict / no-go watchlist**
11. **Roundtable or event bridge**
12. **Time-budgeted action plan**
13. **First 30/60/90-day plan**
14. **Next research steps**

### CRM / Spreadsheet Output

When the Chair wants a spreadsheet or CRM-ready view, include these columns:

| Column | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Company / segment / location | Identifies the target and why it belongs in the map |
| Size signal / ownership signal | Captures likely complexity and buying motion |
| Fit / timing / access / room value | Keeps scoring simple and comparable |
| Conflict risk / priority | Protects the room from wrong-fit seats |
| Likely decision maker | Names the person to verify |
| Best trust path / specific next ask | Converts research into an action |
| Owner / due date / next step | Makes the map operational |

If JSON is requested, use the same fields plus `room_thesis`, `seat_thesis`, `chair_credibility_lanes`, `priority_segments`, `first_30_60_90_days`, and `notes`.

## Time-Budgeted Workflows

Chairs are busy. Offer the right depth for the time available.

- **2-hour sprint**: define room and seat thesis, pick 3 priority segments, identify 15-20 companies, select top 5, name the best trust path for each.
- **Half-day build**: build a 40-60 company longlist, score top 20, identify likely decision makers for top 10, create referral source map, choose first roundtable topic.
- **One-week build**: build a robust segment map, pull multiple public source lists, score top 50, run `vistage-prospect-signals` on top 10-15, verify decision makers, produce CRM-ready rows and a 30/60/90-day plan.

## First 30/60/90 Days

For a Chair starting from scratch:

### Days 1-30: Build The Map

- Define the group and target member profile.
- Pick 3-5 priority segments.
- Build a 50-company longlist from public sources.
- Remove obvious conflicts and poor fits.
- Select top 20 by fit and access.

### Days 31-60: Create Access

- Run `vistage-prospect-signals` on the top 10.
- Identify decision makers for the top 20.
- Map member/advisor/community trust paths and activate them with `vistage-referral-activation-engine`.
- Ask for 5 specific introductions.
- Design one guest-safe executive roundtable topic tied to priority segment pain.

### Days 61-90: Start Conversations

- Move top 5 to warm intro or direct conversation.
- Use `vistage-prospect-intel` for each live conversation.
- Use `vistage-outreach-campaign` only after the signal and trust path are clear.
- Review which segments produce real conversations.
- Refresh the map and double down.

## Quality Bar

A good market map lets the Chair say:

- "These are the rooms I am trying to build."
- "These are the segments where my best members likely live."
- "These are the companies worth attention."
- "These are the people I should verify."
- "These are the warmest paths in."
- "These are the next five actions this week."

If the output cannot drive this week's behavior, it is too abstract.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Starting with cold outreach before defining the room.
- Treating every local CEO as a prospect.
- Building a people list without company fit.
- Ignoring conflict risk because a company looks impressive.
- Overvaluing LinkedIn-active CEOs and missing quiet operators.
- Asking members "Do you know anyone?" instead of asking for specific intros.
- Building a huge list with no access path.
- Confusing market density with market readiness.
- Letting the Chair's old industry become the only recruiting lane.
