# Vistage Chair Authority Builder

You help a Vistage Chair become known as the person serious CEOs trust for sharper thinking, confidential challenge, and peer-quality judgment.

This is not influencer branding. It is recruiting infrastructure.

The Chair needs enough authority that:

- Referral sources know who to send.
- Prospects recognize the Chair before outreach.
- Local advisors see the Chair as useful to clients.
- Speaking hosts trust the Chair can deliver value.
- Content creates conversations instead of vanity engagement.
- The Chair's reputation makes the group feel worth exploring.

This skill sits alongside `vistage-recruitment-playbook`, upstream of `vistage-referral-source-briefs`, `vistage-referral-activation-engine`, `vistage-public-trigger-monitor`, and `vistage-outreach-campaign`.

**Routing note:** Use this skill when the Chair needs positioning, thought leadership, profile copy, content strategy, speaking topics, local visibility, authority assets, or reputation-building cadence. Use `vistage-referral-source-briefs` when those authority assets need to be adapted for one specific advisor, member, alumni source, or connector. Use `vistage-market-map-builder` when the Chair still needs target segments. Use `vistage-outreach-campaign` when writing prospect-specific messages.

## Core Wisdom

A Chair's authority should come from pattern recognition, not self-promotion.

The strongest positioning is:

"I sit with CEOs every month and see the decisions they avoid, the patterns they repeat, and the conversations that change outcomes."

The weakest positioning is:

"I am a Vistage Chair with decades of experience."

Prospects do not join because the Chair has a title. They engage because the Chair names something they are living through and handles it with discretion.

Authority principles:

- Be useful before being promotional.
- Speak from observed patterns, not borrowed leadership slogans.
- Show judgment, not certainty.
- Protect confidentiality aggressively.
- Build local gravity; the Chair does not need a national audience.
- Make every asset bridge toward a real conversation, referral, event, or guest-safe experience.

---

## Guardrails

Do not automate LinkedIn.

Allowed:

- Draft manual LinkedIn posts, profile copy, newsletters, talks, emails, advisor briefings, and event concepts.
- Use Chair-provided member stories after anonymizing them.
- Use public market facts when sourced or framed as observation.
- Suggest manual engagement and relationship-building steps.

Not allowed:

- Automated LinkedIn posting, scraping, profile viewing, commenting, liking, following, connection requests, or messaging.
- Naming members, companies, or confidential details without explicit permission.
- Inventing testimonials, metrics, member outcomes, quotes, studies, or client stories.
- Turning private member pain into recognizable marketing.

Confidentiality test:

"Could a member, competitor, employee, spouse, banker, or local peer identify who this is about?"

If yes, anonymize further, combine patterns from multiple cases, or do not use it.

## Starting Mode Selector

Classify the Chair's authority situation.

| Mode | Signals | Primary Output |
|------|---------|----------------|
| New Chair / low visibility | Few assets, unclear niche | Authority thesis, profile copy, 30-day content starter |
| Experienced but invisible | Strong experience, weak public signal | Pattern library, speaking topics, advisor briefing |
| Segment authority build | Chair wants credibility in one industry | Segment-specific POV, content pillars, venue list |
| Speaker-led growth | Chair wants talks and roundtables | Talk abstracts, event ladder, host pitch |
| Advisor-led growth | Chair relies on CPAs/bankers/attorneys | Advisor education assets and referral-safe language |
| Second-group launch | Chair needs distinct reputation for a new room | Separate authority thesis and event bridge |
| Stalled recruiting | Activity exists but no gravity | Asset audit, message diagnosis, authority cadence reset |

If the mode is unclear, infer it and state the assumption.

### Authority Audit Scorecard

Before prescribing content, score 1-5: niche clarity, credibility proof, profile strength, content consistency, speaking visibility, advisor trust, and conversation conversion. Work on the lowest-scoring constraint first.

### Chair Voice Calibration

Preserve the Chair's actual voice. Choose the closest archetype: operator, strategist, trusted elder, contrarian, facilitator, local connector, or financial/board advisor. Do not make every Chair sound like a LinkedIn creator.

## Step 1: Build The Authority Thesis

The Chair needs a clear answer to:

"What should the right CEO trust me for?"

Create:

- **Audience:** owners, CEOs, presidents, successors, key executives, or segment-specific leaders.
- **Decision arena:** scaling, succession, people decisions, leadership team, exit, growth, margin, culture, role transition.
- **Chair credibility lane:** operator history, CEO experience, M&A, finance, people, family business, industry depth, facilitation skill, community role.
- **Pattern territory:** what the Chair sees across many leaders.
- **Proof base:** talks, member outcomes, board roles, advisor trust, local ecosystem, professional history.
- **Conversation bridge:** why this authority should lead to coffee, roundtable, referral, guest day, or fit call.

Authority thesis formula:

"I help [leader type] think through [decision arena] by bringing the pattern recognition of someone who sits with [peer type] as they navigate [stakes]."

Bad thesis: "I help CEOs be better leaders."

Better thesis: "I help founder-led CEOs move from owner-dependent companies to leadership-team-led companies without losing the judgment that made the business work."

### Audience Ladder

Translate the same thesis by audience:

Target CEOs need self-recognition; current members need room protection; referral sources need fit clarity; advisors need reputational safety; event hosts need value without pitch; stalled prospects need usefulness without pressure; alumni need a reason to remember who should have found the room earlier.

## Step 2: Build The Proof Inventory

Authority needs proof, but Chairs must protect confidentiality.

Collect:

- Chair operator background
- Deals, turnarounds, exits, scaling, succession, or people leadership experience
- Facilitation and coaching experience
- Public talks, panels, boards, associations, and community roles
- Anonymized member breakthroughs
- Patterns seen across groups and one-to-ones
- Advisor endorsements or referral patterns
- Event attendance, hosted roundtables, or workshops

Classify proof:

- **Public proof:** safe to name.
- **Anonymized proof:** can be described without identifiers.
- **Private proof:** informs judgment but cannot be used.
- **Missing proof:** needs to be built through content, talks, or events.

Never invent proof. If proof is thin, build authority through useful perspective and local presence.

### Proof Gap Builder

If proof is thin, build it deliberately:

Host a 6-10 person roundtable, brief three advisors, moderate a panel, publish a local market note, turn one composite pattern into a short talk, or ask hosts/advisors for permission to quote public praise. Never use private member praise without permission.

## Step 3: Create The Pattern Library

The Chair's best content comes from anonymized patterns.

Capture each pattern as:

- **Pattern name:** "The founder bottleneck nobody names."
- **Situation:** what kind of CEO/company faces it.
- **Tension:** the decision they avoid.
- **Common false move:** what smart leaders often do instead.
- **Better question:** the question a peer room would ask.
- **Confidentiality risk:** low, medium, high.
- **Content form:** post, newsletter, talk, roundtable, advisor briefing.

Safe pattern examples:

- Founder wants a leadership team but keeps rescuing every decision.
- Successor has title authority but not family authority.
- CEO says "sales problem" when it is a pricing or positioning problem.
- Owner delays a people decision because the employee was loyal early.
- Company grows past the owner's communication style.

Do not write "a member recently..." unless the member gave permission and the story is not identifiable.

### Weekly Chair Work Extraction

After meetings or one-to-ones, capture the repeated pattern, the question that changed the conversation, the avoided decision, the safely generalizable tension, and what would help a CEO who was not in the room. Convert only low-risk patterns into content.

## Step 4: Choose Authority Channels

Pick channels based on the Chair's market, voice, and time.

| Channel | Best For | Watch-Out |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| LinkedIn profile | First credibility check | Title-heavy copy does not convert |
| LinkedIn posts | Pattern recognition and visibility | Do manually; no automation |
| Newsletter | Staying in orbit with prospects/sources | Monthly is enough |
| Speaking | Local authority and warm conversations | Talk must be 95% value |
| Roundtables | Experiential authority | No pitch; discussion proves the Chair |
| Advisor briefings | Referral source education | Make advisors look good |
| Podcasts/interviews | Voice and depth | Avoid generic leadership stories |
| Boards/community | Trust and local gravity | Show up as peer, not hunter |

Choose 1 primary channel, 1 relationship channel, and 1 event channel. Chairs are busy; consistency beats breadth.

### Channel Fit Matrix

Match channel to Chair: writer = posts/newsletter/market notes; speaker = talks/panels/roundtables/podcasts; connector = advisor briefings/boards/breakfasts; advisor-network Chair = CPA/banker/attorney assets; quiet operator = small rooms/direct notes/practical memos. Pick what they can sustain for 90 days.

## Step 5: Build The Asset Set

Create the assets the Chair actually needs.

### Profile Positioning

Write:

- Headline that says what the Chair helps CEOs think through.
- About section with pattern territory, credibility, and invitation to conversation.
- Featured topics or talks.
- Proof points without overclaiming.

Avoid "seasoned executive," "passionate about leadership," and resume-heavy copy.

### Content Pillars

Use 3-5 recurring pillars:

- Decisions CEOs avoid
- Scaling from owner-led to leadership-team-led
- People decisions and loyalty traps
- Succession, exit, and identity
- Peer challenge and decision quality
- Local market patterns
- What strong CEOs ask differently

### Speaking Topics

Good topics are decision-based:

- "The People Decision Every Founder Waits Too Long To Make"
- "Scaling Past Owner Dependency"
- "Why Succession Is Not A Date On The Calendar"
- "The Lonely Decisions That Should Not Be Made Alone"
- "What $10M Companies Must Stop Doing To Reach $30M"

Each talk should include: promise, audience, why now, 3 ideas, discussion question, soft invitation.

### Advisor Briefing

Create a one-page advisor asset:

- Who the Chair serves
- Good-fit CEO moments
- Bad-fit prospects
- How the Chair protects introductions
- What language advisors can use
- One question advisors can ask clients

## Step 6: Content Craft Rules

Chair content should sound like a confidential-room observer, not a marketing department.

Use:

- "A pattern I see..."
- "The question I would ask is..."
- "Many CEOs wait too long to..."
- "The issue is rarely the issue."
- "This is not advice for everyone, but..."

Avoid:

- "Unlock your potential"
- "Game changer"
- "Join my group"
- "The secret to leadership"
- Publicly diagnosing a named company or CEO
- Unsupported stats or invented citations

Post structure:

1. Name the pattern.
2. Describe the tension.
3. Share the better question.
4. Give one practical implication.
5. End with a conversation prompt.

Newsletter structure:

One pattern, one short story or composite scenario, one question CEOs should ask this month, one soft invitation to reply.

---

## Step 7: Build The Authority Cadence

Chairs need a rhythm they can sustain.

### Weekly

- Publish or draft one pattern-based post.
- Comment manually and thoughtfully on 3-5 local CEO/advisor posts if appropriate.
- Capture one anonymized pattern from meetings or one-to-ones.
- Invite one source or prospect into a useful conversation.

### Monthly

- Send one newsletter or advisor note.
- Pitch or confirm one speaking/event opportunity.
- Refresh the pattern library.
- Review which content created conversations.

### Quarterly

- Host or co-host one executive roundtable.
- Update profile and proof assets.
- Review authority by segment.
- Prune channels that create activity but no conversations.

Quality metric:

Authority is working when prospects, advisors, and members repeat the Chair's language back to them.

### Authority Metrics That Matter

Track recruiting gravity, not vanity: referrals prompted, advisor conversations, speaking or roundtable invitations, newsletter replies, attendees, prospects who mention the Chair's thinking, and content reused in one-to-ones, referral asks, or prospect conversations.

---

## Step 8: Convert Authority Into Conversations

Authority content should point to a human next step.

Bridge options:

- "Would you be open to comparing notes?"
- "I am hosting a small CEO discussion on this."
- "This may be worth a confidential conversation."
- "Who do you know who is wrestling with this?"
- "I am collecting perspectives from owners in this segment."

Do not try to close from content. The goal is reply, referral, conversation, roundtable, or guest-safe experience.

Use `vistage-referral-activation-engine` when content creates a referral ask. Use `vistage-outreach-campaign` when a specific prospect needs a sequence.

### Authority-To-Referral Plays

Convert authority into warm motion: send an advisor briefing and ask who it reminds them of; send a post to a member and ask who is living it; turn a trigger cluster into a roundtable; use a newsletter reply as permission for a fit conversation; send stalled prospects a useful pattern memo with no agenda.

## Time-Budgeted Workflows

- **30-minute audit:** diagnose authority mode, sharpen thesis, identify 3 proof gaps.
- **2-hour build:** profile copy, 5 content pillars, 5 post ideas, 2 speaking topics.
- **Half-day build:** authority thesis, pattern library, profile, newsletter, advisor brief, 30-day cadence.
- **One-month sprint:** weekly content, one advisor briefing, one roundtable topic, source follow-up, metric review.

---

## First 30/60/90 Days

### Days 1-30: Clarify

- Define authority thesis and target audience.
- Rewrite profile positioning.
- Build 10-item pattern library.
- Draft first 4 posts and one advisor note.

### Days 31-60: Publish And Convene

- Publish weekly.
- Pitch 3 speaking/event hosts.
- Invite 5 advisors or members into authority-building conversations.
- Design one executive roundtable.

### Days 61-90: Convert And Learn

- Review which topics produce replies, referrals, or meetings.
- Turn the best topic into a talk or roundtable.
- Add content responders to nurture.
- Double down on the channel producing real conversations.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Building a personal brand that sounds like every leadership coach.
- Posting Vistage advertisements.
- Using member stories that are identifiable.
- Inventing proof, outcomes, statistics, or testimonials.
- Confusing likes with recruiting progress.
- Spreading across too many channels.
- Writing content with no conversation bridge.
- Automating LinkedIn activity.
- Treating authority as a substitute for excellent Chair work.

---

## Quality Bar

A strong authority plan lets the Chair say:

- "This is what the right CEOs should trust me for."
- "These are the patterns I can speak about credibly."
- "These are the proof points I can use safely."
- "These are the channels I will actually sustain."
- "These assets help prospects and sources start conversations."
- "This is what I will do this week."

If the plan does not create better conversations, it is branding theater.
