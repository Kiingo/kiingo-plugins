## Part 1: The 12-Month Strategic Curriculum

### Why This Matters More Than Chairs Think

Your 12-month theme arc is not content planning. It's your competitive moat. Most Chairs plan month-to-month: "What should we talk about in March?" This produces a random walk â€” each meeting feels disconnected, there's no compounding value, and after 18 months members can't articulate what makes this group different from any other.

The best Chairs design an arc where Month 6 is impossible without Months 1-5, where every theme builds decision muscle in a specific area, and where by December members can point to 15+ decisions they've made and tracked across the year. That's a group with a 95% renewal rate and a waiting list.

### Inputs Needed

1. **Industry mix**: What sectors? Manufacturing, services, healthcare, software, construction, etc.
2. **Group's biggest shared challenge**: Margin pressure? Talent? Growth plateau? Succession?
3. **Fiscal year timing**: Calendar year? June FY? This determines when to place planning and budget themes.
4. **Group maturity**: New (< 6 months), established (6-24 months), mixed tenure, veteran (2+ years)
5. **Chair's differentiation goal**: What do you want to be known for? "The group where CEOs make hard decisions and follow through" is different from "The group where leaders develop emotional intelligence."

### Curriculum Design Principles

**1. Compounding value.** Each month's outputs feed the next. If you do pricing in Month 3, the decisions members made should inform the capacity planning discussion in Month 5 and the cash management review in Month 7. A random sequence of good topics is worse than a mediocre sequence that compounds.

**2. Business cycle alignment.** Place themes when they're most actionable:
- Annual planning: October-November (before budgets finalize)
- Pricing strategy: January-February (new year, clean slate to test)
- Talent review: February-March (anuual review season, bonus decisions)
- Mid-year recalibration: June-July (half the year is data)
- Cash/financial: Q4 (year-end optimization, planning capex)
- Strategic clarity: January (fresh start) or post-Q2 (enough data to assess)

A pricing exercise in July is academic. A pricing exercise in January with a "test by March 1" commitment is actionable. Timing is half the battle.

**3. Decision density.** If a theme can't produce 2-3 specific, datable, measurable commitments per member, it's not a theme â€” it's a TED talk. "Leadership" is not a theme. "Which people decision are you avoiding and when will you make it?" is a theme.

**4. Industry translation.** Same framework, different applications. When you teach pricing: the manufacturer thinks about SKU rationalization. The SaaS CEO thinks about tier restructuring. The contractor thinks about change order policy. The services firm thinks about hourly-to-value-based pricing. Show how the same decision framework applies to each.

**5. Lifecycle sensitivity.** New members (month 1 in the group) need on-ramps. Veterans (month 30) need advanced challenges. Design dual-track: the exercise has a basic version and an advanced version. New members answer "What's your biggest pricing challenge?" Veterans answer "Where is your pricing 20% below what your value justifies, and what's stopping you from fixing it?"

## Part 2: Icebreakers & Check-In Questions

### Why This Matters

The opening 10 minutes of a meeting determine the next 5 hours. A great check-in does three things: transitions members from their busy day to being present, creates relational connection, and gives the Chair a temperature read on the room.

### Principles

- **Never cheesy.** CEOs have zero tolerance for infantilizing activities. Every question should feel like something a thoughtful leader would genuinely find interesting.
- **Match the moment.** Deep vulnerability questions in a new group backfire. Surface questions in an advanced group waste the trust they've built.
- **Connect to theme when possible.** Prime the brain for the day's work.
- **Keep it tight.** 30-60 seconds per person. 12 members = 8-12 minutes.
- **Chair goes first.** Model the depth you want. If you want vulnerability, be vulnerable. If you want brevity, be brief.

### Question Library

**Level 1 â€” Light (new groups, warm-up, low energy days):**
- "One word for your week."
- "What's consuming your mental energy right now â€” doesn't have to be business?"
- "Best meal in the last month. Go."
- "What's the best piece of advice you've ignored?"
- "What did you read, watch, or listen to recently that stuck?"

**Level 2 â€” Reflective (established groups, building depth):**
- "What decision are you sitting with that you haven't made?"
- "When were you last genuinely surprised by your business?"
- "What does your team do well that you don't take enough credit for enabling?"
- "What did you say 'no' to recently â€” and was it right?"
- "What did you believe 5 years ago about business that you no longer believe?"

**Level 3 â€” Deep (advanced groups, high trust):**
- "Where are you currently fooling yourself?"
- "What's the conversation you keep putting off?"
- "What would you do differently if you weren't afraid of what people would think?"
- "What about your leadership do you know needs to change but you haven't changed?"
- "What does the next version of you as a leader look like?"

**Level 4 â€” Theme-Connected (prime for the day's work):**
- Pricing: "When was the last time a customer said your price was too high â€” were they right?"
- Talent: "If you could clone one person on your team, who and why?"
- Strategy: "What's one thing you do that you should stop?"
- Cash: "What keeps you up at night about your balance sheet?"
- Innovation: "What will be completely different about your business in 3 years?"
- Leadership: "What's the hardest truth someone told you recently â€” were they right?"

**Seasonal:**
- January: "One word for what you want this year to be about."
- Post-holiday: "What clarity did you get during time off?"
- Year-end: "What's the one thing you accomplished this year that surprised you?"
- Summer: "Are you taking real time off? What would need to be true for you to disconnect?"

### Format Variations

- **One-Word Round:** "One word for your state right now." Fast (2 min). Good temperature.
- **High/Low:** "High and low since we last met." More depth (5-6 min). Surfaces who needs support.
- **Scale:** "1-10 on energy/confidence/clarity." Quantitative. Tracks patterns over time.
- **Paired Check-In:** "Turn to the person next to you. 2 minutes each. Share what's really on your mind." More intimate. Good when group needs connection before content.

## Part 3: Member Lifecycle & Group Health

### Lifecycle Stages

**Onboarding (Months 1-3):**
They're assessing: "Is this worth my time?" They need quick wins, feeling of belonging, understanding the norms. Risk: feeling lost, intimidated by veterans. Chair: pair with a veteran buddy, check in after every meeting, celebrate their first contribution publicly.

**Practitioner insight:** The biggest onboarding failure is assuming new members will "figure it out." They won't. Someone has to explicitly teach them: how issue processing works, what the commitment protocol looks like, that it's OK to be vulnerable, and that confidentiality is absolute. A 30-minute onboarding call before their first meeting prevents 6 months of confusion.

**Engagement (Months 4-12):**
They're deepening. They need increasing challenge, real breakthroughs, peer relationships forming. Risk: plateauing if the Chair doesn't push in one-to-ones. Chair: push harder, celebrate follow-through, deepen accountability.

**Peak Value (Months 12-24):**
They're contributing at a high level. Risk: becoming the "expert" who gives advice but stops receiving. Chair: challenge their assumptions, bring in new stimuli, ask them to mentor newer members.

**Renewal Risk (Months 24+):**
They're evaluating: "Am I still growing?" Risk: "I've heard it all before." Chair: fresh theme angles, advanced exercises, ask for their input on curriculum, give them a leadership role in the group.

**The renewal conversation:** Don't wait until renewal is due. 90 days before: "What would make next year even more valuable than this year?" This surfaces issues while there's time to address them.

### Group Health Indicators

**Leading (predict problems):**
- Pre-work completion dropping (< 70% = concern)
- Attendance slipping (< 85% = concern)
- Same people dominating every month
- Commitments getting vaguer
- Side conversations during meeting
- Phones out more than usual

**Lagging (confirm problems):**
- Renewal rate dropping (< 80% = serious)
- Referrals drying up
- Follow-through declining (< 60% = accountability is broken)
- Energy trending down 3+ months
- One-to-one cancellations increasing

### Intervention Playbook

**Engagement dropping:**
1. Survey: "What would make this more valuable?"
2. Refresh formats (use Exercise Lab skill)
3. Guest speaker to break the pattern
4. Redesign next quarter based on what they actually need
5. Honest one-to-ones: "I'm noticing you seem less engaged. What's happening?"

**Trust damaged (confidentiality breach, conflict):**
1. Address directly â€” jnever ignore
2. Restate norms explicitly
3. Create opportunity for repair (conversation, not just apology)
4. Monitor 2-3 meetings closely
5. If trust doesn't rebuild, consider whether the member should stay

**Silent member:**
Call within a week: "I bvcn't heard from you. What's going on?" Listen for: business crisis, personal issue, group dissatisfaction, or quiet exit. Offer support or adjustment.

**The hard conversation:** Sometimes a member needs to leave â€” bad fit, toxic behavior, or persistent confidentiality issues. This is one of the hardest Chair decisions. But keeping a wrong-fit member damages the other 11. One honest conversation with the wrong member is better than 11 members wondering why you won't act.

## Part 4: Chair Practice Strategy

Every Chair should answer in one sentence: "What's different about my group?" A Chair known for "the group where CEOs make hard decisions and follow through" has a waiting list. A Chair known for "nice group, good discussions" has churn.

Your curriculum IS your differentiation. The themes you choose, the frameworks you teach, the accountability you enforce, and the results your members produce â€” that's your brand.

4. **The second group** â€” Once you've mastered one, launch another. Your curriculum transfers. Your reputation compounds. But don't launch too early â€” a mediocre second group dilutes your brand.
