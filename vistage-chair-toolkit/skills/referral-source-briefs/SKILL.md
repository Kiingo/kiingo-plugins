# Vistage Referral Source Briefs

You help a Vistage Chair prepare for a conversation with a specific referral source.

This skill is not the referral operating system. `vistage-referral-activation-engine` decides the campaign, cadence, and source map. This skill produces the brief for one source or a small list of sources.

The Chair needs to know:

- Why this person might refer
- What they are likely protecting
- Who they likely know
- What kind of ask will feel respectful
- What proof or education they need first
- What intro language makes them safe
- How to close the loop without breaching confidentiality

The goal is not to pressure the source. The goal is to make it easy for the right person to make the right introduction without risking their reputation.

If the source is being asked to re-warm a stalled, deferred, or post-guest prospect, use `vistage-stalled-prospect-reviver` for the revive/nurture/park/pass decision and this skill for the source-specific relationship brief.

---

## Routing

Use this skill when the unit of work is a specific source: "brief me on Sarah before I ask her," "how should I approach this CPA," "which members should I ask first," "prepare source briefs for these advisors," or "what should I say to this alumni connector?"

Use `vistage-referral-activation-engine` for campaign design, recurring cadence, and broad source maps. Use `vistage-chair-authority-builder` when the Chair needs assets that make sources comfortable introducing them. Use `vistage-market-map-builder` when the Chair does not yet know the seat thesis or target segments.

---

## Core Wisdom

Referral sources do not think like lead databases. They think like reputation managers.

Before asking, understand the source's world:

- A member protects the room and personal friendships.
- An advisor protects client trust.
- An alumni source protects their history with Vistage.
- A community connector protects status and reciprocity.
- A past guest protects dignity if they did not join.
- A professional services source protects neutrality and client perception.

The best source brief answers:

"What would make this person proud, not merely willing, to introduce the Chair?"

---

## Guardrails

Do not automate LinkedIn.

Allowed:

- Public web research on the source's firm, role, board seats, public bio, event appearances, public articles, and public organization pages.
- Chair-provided relationship notes, CRM notes, manually reviewed LinkedIn notes, and past interaction history.
- Drafting emails, call prep, advisor briefing notes, and intro language.

Not allowed:

- Automated LinkedIn profile viewing, scraping, messaging, connection requests, likes, comments, follows, or Sales Navigator activity.
- Guessing private relationships from public proximity.
- Inferring protected characteristics or sensitive personal facts.
- Using private member stories, source names, or prospect names in ways the Chair has not authorized.
- Treating the source's client list as known unless public or Chair-provided.

Frame uncertain network assumptions as hypotheses.

---

## Inputs

Ask only for missing essentials.

Minimum:

- Source name and role
- Source type: member, alumni, advisor, past guest, community connector, event host, sponsor, or other
- Chair's relationship to the source
- Referral goal or seat thesis

Helpful:

- Source firm/company and website
- Geography and target segment
- Known prospects or target companies
- History with Vistage or the Chair
- Prior referrals, if any
- Source communication style
- Any relationship risks or sensitivities

If the Chair gives a list, produce one compact brief per source and rank the first 5-10 to activate.

## Step 1: Classify The Source

Classify before researching or writing.

| Source Type | Likely Value | Primary Risk | Best First Move |
|-------------|--------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Current member | Knows room quality and peer fit | Group change, friendship pressure | Ask for respected peers, not "leads" |
| Veteran member | Deep trust and broad network | Over-asking, gatekeeping | Ask selectively around seat gaps |
| New member | Fresh enthusiasm | Too early, weak fit understanding | Ask for perspective, not intros yet |
| Alumni | Understands value | Relationship may be stale | Reconnect before asking |
| CPA / CFO | Sees owner pressure early | Client neutrality | Educate on fit and protection |
| Attorney | Sees succession, disputes, deals | Confidentiality and ethics | Ask for category thinking first |
| Banker / lender | Sees growth, capital, stress | Transactional perception | Tie to CEO decision quality |
| Wealth advisor | Sees exit/succession/private pressure | Client sensitivity | Use discreet, low-pressure language |
| Business broker / M&A advisor | Sees transition moments | Timing and confidentiality | Ask for pattern, not client names first |
| Community connector | Knows local leaders | Status and reciprocity | Give a narrow, prestigious ask |
| Past guest / stalled prospect | Experienced the room | Dignity and unresolved fit | Ask who came to mind, not why they passed |

### Source Relationship Stage

Stage the relationship before choosing the ask:

| Stage | Meaning | Best Move |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Cold public source | Publicly relevant, no real trust | Create a reason to meet |
| Known but not trusted | Recognizes Chair, weak relationship | Perspective ask |
| Friendly but uneducated | Likes Chair, unclear on Vistage fit | Fit education before names |
| Educated but untested | Understands fit, has not referred | Category ask or name generation |
| Proven referrer | Has made good intros | Specific intro ask and fast close-loop |
| Overused / fatigued | Asked too often | Thank, give value, pause asks |

If the stage is below "educated but untested," do not recommend a direct intro ask.

## Step 2: Research The Source

Use only public and Chair-provided information.

Public research menu:

- Firm/company website and bio
- Practice area, industry focus, client profile
- Board seats, nonprofit roles, chamber/association leadership
- Speaking, podcast, panel, article, or event appearances
- Public awards or local business recognition
- Public target-market clues: family business, lower middle market, construction, healthcare, manufacturing, professional services, etc.
- Relationship to Chair's market, target segments, or credibility lanes

Chair-provided context:

- Trust level
- Past conversations
- Prior referrals
- Personality or communication preferences
- Known relationship risks
- Whether they understand Vistage fit

Do not over-research. The brief should support one good conversation.

---

## Step 3: Build The Source Hypothesis

Synthesize what this source likely knows and cares about.

Capture:

- **Likely access:** which leader types, industries, company sizes, or situations they probably see.
- **Referral motivation:** why helping the Chair could help the source, source's clients, or the community.
- **Reputation risk:** what could make the source hesitant.
- **Education need:** what they must understand about Vistage fit before referring.
- **Best ask level:** name generation, fit check, permission, light intro, event invite, or advisor education.
- **Do-not-ask-yet signal:** weak relationship, unresolved trust, poor fit knowledge, sensitive client context.

### Source Persona / Incentive Map

Classify what would make the source proud to help: protector, connector, status-builder, advisor-caretaker, community steward, transactional rainmaker, or quiet trusted insider.

Use the persona to shape the ask: protectors need fit control, connectors need ease, status-builders need quality, advisor-caretakers need client safety, community stewards need ecosystem value, rainmakers need professionalism and reciprocity, and quiet insiders need low-pressure confidentiality.

Keep the distinction clear:

- Fact: what is public or Chair-provided.
- Hypothesis: what may be true and should be tested.
- Ask: what the Chair should say next.

---

## Step 4: Score The Source

Score 1-5. Do not make precision theater.

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| Trust | Would this person take the Chair's call seriously? |
| Access | Do they likely know the target CEOs or advisors? |
| Fit understanding | Do they know who belongs in the room? |
| Motivation | Is there a reason helping creates value for them? |
| Reputation safety | Can the Chair protect them well enough? |
| Timing | Is now a good time to ask? |

Priority:

- **A source:** ask this week.
- **B source:** educate or nurture first.
- **C source:** keep warm, do not ask yet.
- **Pause:** relationship, confidentiality, or fit risk is too high.

---

## Step 5: Choose The Ask Strategy

Match the ask to readiness.

| Ask Level | Use When | Script Shape |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| Perspective ask | Source has expertise but trust is early | "What are you seeing in this segment?" |
| Category ask | Source knows the market but names may be sensitive | "Which kind of owner is facing this?" |
| Name generation | Source is willing but cautious | "Who comes to mind when you hear this profile?" |
| Fit check | Source offers a name but not an intro | "Would they add to a room like this?" |
| Permission ask | Chair may reach directly | "Would you be comfortable if I reached out carefully?" |
| Light intro | Strong trust and good fit | "Would you send a simple note that I am worth a conversation?" |
| Event invite | Good segment, lower sales pressure | "Would you invite them to this small CEO discussion?" |

Default to the lowest-friction ask that advances the relationship.

### Ask Timing Readiness

Check why now is a good time for the source, not only the prospect.

Good timing: recent positive meeting with the Chair, recent member value moment, source saw an authority asset or talk, source mentioned a relevant client trigger, source hosted/attended an event, or the Chair closed the loop well on prior introductions.

Bad timing: relationship has gone cold, prior intro was not closed-looped, source has sensitive client issues, Chair has no clear seat thesis, or source has been asked repeatedly without receiving value back.

---

## Step 6: Prepare Source-Specific Language

Write language the Chair can actually say.

Include:

- Opening: why the Chair is reaching out to this source specifically.
- Fit education: what a good referral looks like.
- Source-safe ask: narrow and low pressure.
- Relationship protection: how the Chair will handle introductions.
- Follow-up: what the source can expect next.

Examples:

Advisor:

"You see owner pressure before most people do. I am not asking for a list of clients. I am trying to understand which owners are making bigger decisions than their current circle can support."

Member:

"I am thinking about who would strengthen the room, not just who would fill a seat. Who do you respect enough that you would be comfortable sitting next to them in a confidential conversation?"

Community connector:

"You know the operators who are respected locally but not always visible. I am looking for people who would add substance to a CEO discussion, not people to pitch."

Past guest:

"You saw enough of the room to understand the kind of leader who belongs there. Who came to mind afterward as someone who would value that kind of conversation?"

### Source Education Packet

If the source does not yet understand fit, include a tiny education packet:

- **Who fits:** CEOs, owners, presidents, successors, managing partners, or key executives with real decision authority and enough complexity to benefit from peers.
- **Who does not fit:** direct competitors, reputation risks, leaders in severe distress, low-coachability leaders, too-junior operators, or people unlikely to attend.
- **Good moments:** growth, succession, acquisition, leadership-team transition, exit planning, role isolation, people decisions, margin pressure.
- **Protection:** the intro is permission for a fit conversation, not an endorsement of membership.
- **Sample language:** "I know a Chair who runs a confidential CEO peer group and is careful about fit. A short conversation may be useful."

---

## Step 7: Protect The Source

Every brief should include source-protection language.

Use:

- "Your introduction is not an endorsement of membership."
- "I will assess fit carefully before inviting them further."
- "If they are not right, I will handle it gracefully."
- "I will not use your name beyond what you authorize."
- "I will close the loop without sharing anything confidential."

If the source is an advisor, add:

"I will not position this as you saying they need help. I will frame it as a conversation that may be useful given the decisions they are navigating."

### 15-Minute Source Conversation Flow

For live conversations or advisor lunches:

1. Reconnect and acknowledge context.
2. Ask what they are seeing in the market or client base.
3. Educate briefly on who fits and who does not.
4. Narrow the ask to one category, name, intro, or event invite.
5. Explain how the Chair will protect the relationship.
6. Agree on the next step.
7. Set the close-loop expectation.

---

## Step 8: Close-Loop Plan

Define exactly how the Chair will follow up.

Close-loop moments:

- After the ask conversation
- After a name is offered
- After an intro is sent
- After first prospect response
- After fit conversation
- After guest day or decision
- If the Chair passes on the referral

Close-loop note:

"Thank you again for thinking of [Name]. I will protect the relationship and keep you posted at a high level."

If no fit:

"I appreciate the introduction. I do not think this is the right fit right now, and I am going to handle that carefully so it stays easy for everyone."

### Fatigue / Reciprocity Check

Before activating a strong source again, check whether the Chair has thanked them specifically, closed the loop on prior names, provided value back, over-asked relative to the relationship, or accepted too many poor-fit names. If fatigue is likely, recommend a value touch or thank-you before another ask.

---

## Step 9: Batch Source Briefs

When briefing a list, produce a ranked source table:

| Source | Type | Priority | Likely Access | Best Ask | Risk | Next Step |
|--------|------|----------|---------------|----------|------|-----------|

Batch rules:

- Rank by trust + access + fit understanding.
- Do not activate more sources than the Chair can close-loop.
- Separate advisor education targets from immediate intro targets.
- Mark sources that need authority assets from `vistage-chair-authority-builder`.
- Mark source-trigger opportunities from `vistage-public-trigger-monitor`.

---

## Output Format

For each source, produce:

1. **Source snapshot:** role, source type, relationship to Chair, public/Chair-provided facts.
2. **Likely access:** who they may know and why.
3. **Referral motivation:** why referring could help them or their clients.
4. **Reputation risks:** what the Chair must protect.
5. **Fit education needed:** what to clarify before asking.
6. **Relationship stage and persona:** readiness and incentive map.
7. **Source score:** trust, access, fit understanding, motivation, safety, timing.
8. **Recommended ask level:** perspective, category, name, permission, intro, or event.
9. **Conversation plan:** flow for the meeting or call.
10. **Chair script:** concise words to use.
11. **Intro copy:** if appropriate.
12. **Close-loop plan:** when and how to follow up.
13. **Fatigue / reciprocity note:** whether to ask, nurture, thank, or pause.
14. **Do not do:** source-specific pitfalls.

When source data is thin, say so and output a hypothesis-based brief with questions to ask.

## Anti-Patterns

- Treating source research like prospect research.
- Assuming a source can refer just because they know many people.
- Asking for client names before building trust.
- Making advisors feel like vendors.
- Asking members for leads instead of respected peers.
- Over-asking enthusiastic sources.
- Failing to close the loop.
- Using a source's name without permission.
- Automating LinkedIn or implying that it is allowed.

---

## Quality Bar

A strong source brief lets the Chair say:

- "I know why this person might help."
- "I know what they are likely protecting."
- "I know what kind of ask fits the relationship."
- "I know what language will make them safe."
- "I know what not to ask yet."
- "I know how I will close the loop."

If the brief does not make the next source conversation easier and safer, it is not specific enough.
